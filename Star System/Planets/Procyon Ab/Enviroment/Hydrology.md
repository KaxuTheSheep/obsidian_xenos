# Erebian Era
## Subsurface Hydrology
During the ante capture period [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] maintains a global subsurface liquid water system beneath a thick planetary ice shell. The surface is frozen across its entirety — a consequence of zero stellar energy input during the rogue phase — but geothermal heat flux from the iron-dominated core is sufficient to keep the full volume of subsurface water liquid beneath the insulating ice layer. This configuration mirrors theoretical models of rogue planet habitability and is consistent with analogous systems observed in ice-covered moons within stellar systems.

Water on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] was acquired primarily through asteroid collisions during the ejection event, supplementing whatever water was present at formation. Post-capture, as surface ice melts under Kairos and [[Procyon B|Lethe]]'s combined energy input, this water will constitute approximately 80% surface coverage — vast shallow seas across the flat basaltic primary crust, with volcanic highlands representing the remaining 20% of exposed surface.

Despite being globally connected, the subsurface water system is biologically inert across most of its extent, as chemical energy gradients sufficient to sustain metabolism exist only within two hydrothermal vent cluster zones. Both vent clusters are located within the same hemisphere but are separated by a significant distance. These two clusters represent the only locations where persistent chemical disequilibrium is sufficient to sustain complex reaction systems on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] during the ante capture period — islands of chemistry in an otherwise energetically dead global ocean.

[[Ferrovita]] occupies the deepest, most chemically aggressive zones of these vent clusters. [[Sulphovita]] occupies the cooler peripheral zones of the same clusters. Despite sharing a physical environment the two domains are chemically isolated from one another by thermal barriers and incompatible fluid chemistry, maintaining independent biospheres throughout the rogue phase.

Post-capture hydraulic reconnection — driven by surface melting, pressure redistribution, and fracture propagation through the ice shell — will eventually bring these isolated biospheres into contact. Given the same-hemisphere proximity of the two vent clusters this contact is expected to occur relatively rapidly on geological timescales, representing the first interaction between the two founding domains of Xenian life.

### Pocket A
The fluid environment of Pocket A is characterised by extremely acidic, metal-dominated conditions tightly coupled to hydrothermal vent interfaces. Chemical processes are controlled primarily by interactions between fluid and mineral surfaces rather than bulk solution chemistry.

#### **Solvent**
- Water — dominant solvent, ~70–80% of fluid volume
- Sulphuric acid — high concentration, pH 1–2, ~10–100 mmol/L equivalent free acidity

#### **Dissolved Gases**
Maintained in solution under extreme pressure:

- Hydrogen (H₂) — ~20–40 mmol/L, primary electron donor
- Hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) — ~20–30 mmol/L
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — low (~5–10 mmol/L)
- Methane (CH₄) — trace to negligible

#### **Dissolved Minerals**
- Iron (Fe²⁺) — **very high concentrations**, ~100–300 mmol/L, (Fe³⁺) — lower concentration due to a lot of reduction chemicals, ~2–8 mmol/L
- Sulphate (SO₄²⁻) — ~20–50 mmol/L
- Dissolved metal ions (Ni, Cu, Zn) — trace but significant for catalysis

#### **pH and Solvent Character**
Bulk fluid pH ranges from 0–2 — strongly acidic and chemically aggressive. High ionic strength and metal concentration limit the stability of complex organic molecules, favouring mineral-mediated reaction pathways.

#### **Fluid Dynamics and Structure**
- Flow is **laminar to slow-moving** within vent structures
- Strong gradients exist at **fluid–rock interfaces**
- Minimal large-scale mixing compared to Pocket B

#### **Energy Structure**
Energy is concentrated in **steep, localized chemical gradients** formed where reduced vent fluids meet more oxidised surroundings.

These gradients are:
- Spatially stable
- Structurally constrained
- Strongly tied to mineral surfaces
Chemical disequilibrium is continuously maintained by vent flux, preventing the system from reaching equilibrium despite local stability.

#### **Defining Characteristics**
- Chemistry dominated by **iron redox cycling**
- Reactions occur primarily at **solid–fluid interfaces**
- Environment is:
    - Highly stable locally
    - Extremely hostile to complex free-floating chemistry

#### **Biological Modification (Erebian Era)**
Biological activity by [[Ferrovita]] and its descendant [[Ferrovita Novus]] introduces a progressive chemical shift in Pocket A fluid over geological timescales. These organisms operate exclusively through reductive metabolisms, systematically altering the redox balance of the environment.

- **Fe³⁺ depletion** — continuous reduction of rock-bound and dissolved Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺ lowers the availability of oxidised iron in active zones, particularly in vent margins
- **Fe²⁺ accumulation** — reduced iron accumulates in solution, increasing the overall reduced character of the fluid
- **Hydrogen consumption** — H₂ is steadily drawn down in regions of high biological density, reinforcing local chemical gradients
- **Methane production** — with the emergence of [[Ferrovita Novus]], methanogenesis (H₂ + CO₂ → CH₄ + H₂O) becomes a significant process, leading to gradual accumulation of dissolved methane in Pocket A fluid
- **Carbon dioxide drawdown (localised)** — CO₂ is consumed during methanogenesis, though this effect remains spatially limited due to continuous vent replenishment

These processes do not homogenise the environment. Instead, they intensify existing gradients:

- Vent cores remain dominated by abiotic chemistry
- Peripheral zones become increasingly biologically modified

The net effect is a **progressive shift toward a more reduced chemical environment**, particularly in cooler vent margins where biological activity is most sustained.

### Pocket B
The fluid environment of Pocket B is characterised by moderately acidic, chemically diverse conditions maintained by a combination of volcanic outgassing, serpentinisation, and continuous low-scale turbulence driven by thermal gradients and vent outflow.

#### **Solvent**
- Water — dominant solvent, ~85–90% of fluid volume
- Sulphuric acid — dissolved, pH 3–4, concentration ~0.1–10 mmol/L

#### **Dissolved Gases**
Maintained in solution by high subsurface pressure:
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — ~20–30 mmol/L, volcanic waste product
- Hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) — ~15–25 mmol/L, primary biological energy compound
- Methane (CH₄) — ~10–20 mmol/L, secondary biological energy compound
- Hydrogen (H₂) — ~5–10 mmol/L, produced by serpentinisation

#### **Dissolved Minerals**
- Sulphate (SO₄²⁻) — ~50–100 mmol/L, dominant electron acceptor in biological redox chemistry
- Iron (Fe²⁺) — ~15–25 mmol/L, (Fe³⁺) — ~10–20 mmol/L, significantly lower than [[Geology#Pocket A|Pocket A]]
- Silicates — trace concentrations

#### **pH and Solvent Character**
Bulk fluid pH sits between 3–4 — moderately acidic, chemically active, and stable enough to support peptide-based biochemistry. The relatively high sulphate concentration is the defining geochemical feature of Pocket B, providing the electron acceptor that underpins [[Sulphovita]]'s sulphur-coupled methane oxidation pathway.

#### **Energy Structure**
Chemical energy is distributed across diffuse and transient gradients generated by continuous mixing of reduced and oxidised species, rather than being confined to stable interfaces. Unlike Pocket A, disequilibrium here is maintained not by persistent vent flux at fixed points but by continuous fluid movement and chemical recombination — a dynamic system that never fully stabilises but never fully exhausts itself either.

#### **Biological Modification (Erebian Era)**
Biological activity by [[Sulphovita]] introduces a dynamic and continuously shifting chemical overprint on Pocket B fluid. Unlike Pocket A, these effects do not drive the system toward a single redox state but instead maintain and amplify chemical disequilibrium.

- **Hydrogen sulphide depletion (localised)** — H₂S is continuously consumed as a primary electron donor, requiring constant replenishment from vent outflow
- **Sulphate consumption** — SO₄²⁻ is used as the terminal electron acceptor in both sulphide and methane oxidation pathways, creating local depletion zones
- **Elemental sulphur and intermediate accumulation** — incomplete oxidation leads to buildup of S⁰ and thiosulphate (S₂O₃²⁻), increasing chemical heterogeneity
- **Methane consumption** — CH₄ is oxidised to CO₂, reducing methane concentrations in biologically active regions
- **Carbon dioxide accumulation** — CO₂ is produced as a metabolic waste product, contributing to slight local acidification
- **Secondary hydrogen sulphide production** — methane oxidation regenerates H₂S, partially feeding back into sulphide oxidation pathways

These processes produce a system characterised by:

- Rapid chemical turnover
- Persistent non-equilibrium conditions
- Spatially and temporally shifting gradients

The net effect is not directional but **dynamic** — Pocket B becomes increasingly chemically complex, with biological activity maintaining a constantly fluctuating balance between reduced and oxidised sulphur species.
