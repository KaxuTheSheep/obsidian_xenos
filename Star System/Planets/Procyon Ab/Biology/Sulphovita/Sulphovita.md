### Overview
Sulphovita emerged in the peripheral zones of hydrothermal vent systems—regions that are cooler relative to vent cores, chemically diverse, and dominated by dilute sulphuric compounds rather than extreme metal-rich conditions. Although it coexists spatially with [[Ferrovita]], it represents a completely independent origin of life with fundamentally different molecular architecture and evolutionary strategy.
### **Genetics**
Peptide-based, modular, and distributed.
Sulphovita does not use nucleic acids or rigid mineral templates. Instead, information is encoded in **short, reusable peptide motifs** that function simultaneously as structural units and catalytic agents. These motifs do not form a single continuous genome; rather, they exist as a **dynamic network of interacting sequences**.
Replication occurs through **structural templating**. Existing peptide motifs catalyse the formation of similar motifs by stabilising intermediate structures during synthesis. Fidelity is not absolute—variation is inherent and continuous.
There is no strict separation between “genetic material” and “metabolic machinery.” The same molecules that encode functional patterns also perform the chemistry required to sustain and reproduce the system. As a result, Sulphovita does not possess a fixed genome in the traditional sense, but instead maintains a **shifting biochemical identity** defined by the current distribution of active peptide modules.
### **Metabolism**
Dual-pathway and opportunistic.
Sulphovita is capable of exploiting multiple redox pathways, primarily:
- Hydrogen sulphide oxidation
- Methane oxidation
#### Hydrogen Sulphide Oxidation
Sulphovita's hydrogen sulphide oxidation pathway uses H₂S as the primary electron donor, extracting energy by moving sulphur through progressive oxidation states. Environmental sulphate (SO₄²⁻) from Pocket B fluid serves as the terminal electron acceptor. The pathway proceeds stepwise:

**H₂S → S⁰ → SO₃²⁻ → SO₄²⁻**

Each step releases electrons which are captured by peptide modules and converted into usable chemical energy. The primary waste products are elemental sulphur (S⁰) and thiosulphate (S₂O₃²⁻), which accumulate in the surrounding fluid rather than being fully cycled back to sulphate internally. The overall reaction does not complete a closed loop — Sulphovita depends on continuous environmental replenishment of both H₂S from vent outflow and SO₄²⁻ from Pocket B bulk fluid to sustain the pathway.

A partial internal sulphur cycle exists — some intermediate sulphur compounds are transiently recycled within the organism's peptide network rather than immediately expelled — but this recycling is incomplete and leaky. Net sulphur flux is always outward, with waste compounds accumulating in Pocket B fluid over time.

##### Stepwise Mechanism
**Step 1 — Sulphide Activation**
H₂S → S⁰ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻
Catalysed by **Sulphide oxidase** — a peptide module that strips the first two electrons from hydrogen sulphide, producing elemental sulphur and releasing protons into solution. This is the most energetically favourable step and the one most likely to initiate spontaneously under Pocket B conditions. Elemental sulphur produced here may be expelled as waste or retained temporarily for further oxidation depending on local sulphate availability.

**Step 2 — Sulphur Oxidation**
S⁰ → SO₃²⁻ + 2e⁻
Catalysed by **Sulphur oxidase** — oxidises elemental sulphur to sulphite, extracting further electrons. This step requires sulphate as an electron acceptor and is therefore sensitive to local SO₄²⁻ concentration. If sulphate availability drops the reaction slows, causing elemental sulphur to accumulate temporarily within or around the organism.

**Step 3 — Sulphite Oxidation**
SO₃²⁻ → SO₄²⁻ + 2e⁻
Catalysed by **Sulphite oxidase** — the terminal oxidation step, converting sulphite to sulphate and extracting the final electrons from the sulphur chain. The sulphate produced here is released into Pocket B fluid, partially replenishing the environmental sulphate pool consumed earlier in the pathway. This partial replenishment is the closest Sulphovita comes to internal sulphur cycling — it is real but insufficient to sustain the pathway without continuous external sulphate input.

##### Pathway Coupling, Switching, and Energy Losses
Hydrogen sulphide oxidation serves a dual role in Sulphovita's metabolism — it is both an independent energy source and the activation primer for methane oxidation. Energy captured during sulphide oxidation is partially redirected to drive the Methanyl activase step of the methane pathway, making the two systems biochemically dependent rather than truly independent.

This coupling creates a hierarchical relationship between the pathways. Hydrogen sulphide oxidation is the foundational process — it can run alone and does so whenever H₂S is available regardless of methane concentration. Methane oxidation is the secondary process — it cannot initiate without hydrogen sulphide oxidation running concurrently to supply activation energy.

Pathway switching is therefore not a binary choice but a **concentration-driven gradient response:**

- **High H₂S, high CH₄** — both pathways run simultaneously, maximum energy extraction
- **High H₂S, low CH₄** — hydrogen sulphide oxidation dominates, methane oxidation minimal
- **Low H₂S, high CH₄** — hydrogen sulphide oxidation slows, methane oxidation halts due to insufficient activation energy despite available substrate
- **Low H₂S, low CH₄** — metabolic activity drops to minimum, organism enters a reduced activity state

Energy losses occur at every step of both pathways. Heat dissipation is unavoidable — each electron transfer loses a fraction of its potential energy to thermal output, warming the immediate fluid environment fractionally. Intermediate compounds that escape the organism before being fully processed represent chemical energy losses — elemental sulphur accumulating in Pocket B fluid is the most significant of these, representing incompletely harvested energy that diffuses away rather than being captured.

The partial internal sulphur cycle partially mitigates these losses but cannot eliminate them. Sulphovita's long term survival therefore depends entirely on the continuous supply of fresh chemical substrates from vent outflow — it is an open system, not a self-sustaining one.
#### Methane Oxidation
Sulphovita's methane oxidation pathway is a sulphur-coupled anaerobic process in which methane serves as the primary electron donor and sulphate (SO₄²⁻) serves as the terminal electron acceptor. The overall reaction is:

**CH₄ + SO₄²⁻ → H₂S + CO₂ + H₂O**

In the acidic conditions of [[Hydrology#Pocket B|Pocket B]], bicarbonate intermediate HCO₃⁻ rapidly converts to CO₂, and bisulphide HS⁻ protonates to H₂S. Both waste products are released directly into the surrounding fluid.

##### Activation Dependency
Methane is a chemically stable molecule. The energy barrier required to break the first C–H bond, the activation step, cannot be overcome by ambient chemistry alone. Sulphovita resolves this through **metabolic coupling**: energy released by hydrogen sulphide oxidation is partially captured by peptide modules and redirected to prime methane activation. Methane oxidation therefore cannot initiate or sustain itself independently — it is contingent on concurrent hydrogen sulphide oxidation providing the necessary activation energy.

This coupling has a direct consequence for pathway switching. When hydrogen sulphide is abundant both pathways operate simultaneously. When hydrogen sulphide becomes scarce the activation energy supply diminishes and methane oxidation slows or halts entirely, defaulting the organism back to hydrogen sulphide oxidation alone until sulphide concentrations recover.

##### Stepwise Mechanism
Methane is oxidised through five sequential steps, each catalysed by a dedicated peptide module:

**Step 1 — Methane Activation**
CH₄ → CH₃⁻
Catalysed by **Methanyl activase** — an iron-coordinating peptide module that uses trace Fe²⁺ from Pocket B fluid to destabilise the first C–H bond. This is the energy-costly step primed by hydrogen sulphide oxidation. The iron coordination is critical — without sufficient trace iron in solution this step cannot proceed, making Sulphovita's methane oxidation sensitive to iron availability despite iron not being a primary metabolic resource.

**Step 2 — Methyl Oxidation**
CH₃⁻ → CH₂O
Catalysed by **Methylox transferase** — transfers electrons from the methyl group to the sulphate reduction chain. Produces formaldehyde as an intermediate, which is immediately processed by the next module rather than accumulating.

**Step 3 — Formyl Oxidation**
CH₂O → CHO⁻
Catalysed by **Formyl oxidase** — further oxidation of the carbon intermediate, transferring additional electrons to sulphate. The organism's peptide network coordinates this step with sulphate availability in the surrounding fluid, slowing if sulphate concentration drops below a critical threshold.

**Step 4 — Carbonyl Transfer**
CHO⁻ → CO
Catalysed by **Carbonyl transferase** — produces a brief carbon monoxide intermediate. CO is toxic to most Earth biochemistry but is handled internally within Sulphovita's peptide network, processed immediately rather than accumulating to harmful concentrations.

**Step 5 — Final Hydration**
CO + H₂O → HCO₃⁻ → CO₂
Catalysed by **Carboxyl hydrase** — hydrates the carbon monoxide intermediate to bicarbonate, which under Pocket B's acidic conditions rapidly converts to CO₂ and water. This is the terminal step, completing the oxidation chain and releasing the final waste products.

##### Waste Products and Ecological Implications
The waste products of methane oxidation — H₂S, CO₂, and H₂O — are released continuously into Pocket B fluid. CO₂ accumulation acidifies the local environment slightly, reinforcing the moderately acidic character of Pocket B. H₂S released as waste simultaneously replenishes the hydrogen sulphide pool that primes further methane activation, creating a degree of internal chemical recycling within the pathway coupling system.

### **Membrane**
Sulphonolipid bilayer, flexible and chemically active.
Sulphovita is enclosed by a membrane composed of **sulphonolipids**, analogous to phospholipids but incorporating sulphonate groups instead of phosphate. This structure is:
- Stable in acidic conditions
- Flexible under high pressure
- Chemically compatible with sulphur-rich environments
Unlike passive biological membranes, the Sulphovita membrane participates directly in chemical processes. It acts as a **reactive interface**, facilitating the exchange and transformation of molecules between the organism and its surroundings.
The boundary of the organism is therefore not strictly defined; it is a **zone of active chemistry** rather than a fixed barrier.

### **Ecological Character**
Adaptive, unstable, and chemically integrated.
Sulphovita occupies the transitional regions between extreme vent chemistry and the broader planetary environment. These zones are characterised by:
- Rapid chemical fluctuations
- Mixed energy sources
- Lower but still significant temperatures
In this context, Sulphovita’s defining trait is not efficiency, but **adaptability**. Individual structures may be transient or unstable, but the system persists through continuous reconfiguration.
Rather than maintaining strict individuality, Sulphovita systems may exchange components, merge, or fragment. Identity is therefore **fluid**, and survival operates at the level of chemical networks rather than discrete organisms.
