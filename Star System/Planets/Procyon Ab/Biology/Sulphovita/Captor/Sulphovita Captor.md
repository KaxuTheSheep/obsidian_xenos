### Overview
[[Sulphovita]] Captor is the second major divergent domain of [[Sulphovita]], emerging from peripheral [[Sulphovita Primalis]] Purus populations in zones where sustained high-density Primalis activity has depleted free amino acid availability below viable abiotic replenishment rates. Rather than competing for increasingly scarce free monomers, Captor lineages evolved the capacity to scavenge and process fragmented peptide debris released by dying [[Sulphovita]] populations — the first organism on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] to exploit another organism as a resource. [[Sulphovita Captor Carpens]] represents the most significant evolutionary development of the Erebian Era — the first life on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] to physically interact with another organism, developing engulfment of dead cells as a resource acquisition strategy directly pre-adapting it for predation in the Photian Era.

### Environmental Niche
- **Location:** High density Primalis zones of Pocket B, particularly at population boundaries where free amino acid depletion is most severe
- **Temperature:** ~80–120°C, overlapping with Primalis zones
- **Pressure:** High subsurface, consistent with [[Sulphovita Primalis]] range
- **Chemistry:** Reduced free amino acid availability relative to vent-proximal zones, abundant fragmented peptide debris from Primalis population die-off, otherwise chemically similar to [[Sulphovita Primalis]] zones
- **Energy source:** Follows [[Sulphovita#Metabolism]] — H₂S oxidation primary, methane oxidation coupled secondary. Captor's innovation is in **resource acquisition** rather than energy metabolism.

Captor occupies the same vent-proximal zones as [[Sulphovita Primalis]] but exploits a resource layer invisible to Primalis — the fragmented peptide debris accumulating at population boundaries. This niche does not require Captor to outcompete Primalis for chemical energy substrates but to exploit a secondary resource Primalis generates as waste through its own population dynamics.

### Genetics
Sulphovita Captor uses the base [[Sulphovita#Genetics|Sulphovita genetic architecture]] — peptide-based information encoding, conformational stabilisation as the replication mechanism, and the same variation sources operating across all Sulphovita. Captor's innovations are expressed entirely through novel peptide module types introduced into this existing system rather than any change to the underlying architecture.

Two novel module classes define the domain:

**Surface-bound cleavage modules** — membrane-embedded peptide modules operating at the outer membrane surface, capable of binding fragmented peptide structures from the surrounding fluid and cleaving them into segments compatible with existing amino acid import channels. These modules extend Captor's resource acquisition beyond free amino acids to include biological debris, supplementing and eventually superseding abiotic monomer uptake as the primary source of peptide building material in debris-rich zones.

### Metabolism
Follows [[Sulphovita#Metabolism]] entirely for energy acquisition — H₂S oxidation as primary pathway, methane oxidation as coupled secondary pathway, pathway coupling and switching behaviour unchanged from [[Sulphovita Primalis]].

Captor's metabolic distinction is not in energy chemistry but in **monomer acquisition**. The fragment import and processing machinery described under [[Sulphovita Captor#The Cleavage and Import Mechanism]] supplements and eventually supersedes free amino acid uptake as the primary source of peptide building material, dramatically improving replication efficiency in amino acid-scarce environments.

### Membrane
Follows [[Sulphovita#Membrane]] entirely for baseline sulphonolipid architecture and transport module organisation, with two additions:

**Surface-bound cleavage module** — a membrane-embedded peptide module that binds incoming fragmented peptide structures at the outer membrane surface, cleaves them into segments compatible with existing amino acid import channels, and passes them directly inward. Present in both Legens and Carpens, progressively refined between the two lineages.

**Engulfment capability** — present in Carpens only. The sulphonolipid membrane develops the capacity to extend outward, surround an external target, and fuse around it — enclosing the target within a membrane-bounded internal compartment. See [[Sulphovita Captor#Engulfment Mechanism]].

### Reproduction
Follows [[Sulphovita#Reproduction]] entirely. Improved monomer availability from fragment scavenging directly accelerates replication in Captor lineages — in debris-rich zones Captor replication rates exceed those of Primalis populations competing for the same free amino acid pool, compounding Captor's competitive advantage over generations.

### Dormancy and Death
Follows [[Sulphovita#Dormancy and Death]] for all universal mechanisms.

#### Captor-Specific Dormancy
Captor's dormancy profile is more resilient than Primalis in amino acid-scarce environments. Where Primalis populations enter reproductive arrest and eventual dormancy as free monomer availability declines, Captor lineages can sustain replication on fragment debris alone, remaining metabolically active in conditions that force Primalis into reduced activity states.

Energy metabolism dormancy triggers follow [[Sulphovita]] universals — H₂S depletion and sulphate depletion remain the primary dormancy initiators. Captor has no special resistance to these triggers.

**Carpens-specific detail** — engulfment of dead organisms provides a concentrated internal resource pulse that can sustain Carpens through brief periods of external substrate scarcity. A recently engulfed dead organism represents days to weeks of internal peptide material, buffering Carpens against short-term chemical fluctuation more effectively than any other [[Sulphovita]] lineage.

#### Captor-Specific Death
Death mechanisms follow [[Sulphovita]] universals. Captor faces no unique death risks beyond those documented in [[Sulphovita#Dormancy and Death]]. In amino acid-scarce zones Captor's scavenging capability reduces the peptide network collapse risk that threatens Primalis populations — Captor is significantly less vulnerable to monomer starvation death than the Primalis lineages it coexists with.

### Ecological Character
Opportunistic, debris-dependent, and ecologically novel.

[[Sulphovita]] Captor represents a qualitative shift in Pocket B ecology — the first organism on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]] to exploit another organism as a resource rather than competing purely for abiotic chemical substrates. Its emergence marks the transition from a purely chemotrophic ecosystem to one with a biological resource layer overlaid on the chemical foundation.

The ecological impact of Captor on Pocket B chemistry is indirect but significant. By consuming fragmented peptide debris before it fully dissolves back into the amino acid pool, Captor intercepts a recycling pathway that previously returned molecular components to the free monomer pool available to all [[Sulphovita]]. In high density Captor zones the free amino acid pool is therefore depleted faster than in equivalent zones without Captor — Captor's presence compounds the monomer scarcity that drove its own emergence, creating a self-reinforcing selective pressure toward more efficient scavenging.

Carpens's engulfment capability introduces a further ecological dimension — the first physical interaction between organisms on [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]]. Dead organisms in Carpens-occupied zones are consumed rather than dissolving back into the fluid, removing their molecular components from the broader chemical environment entirely rather than recycling them. The long-term ecological consequence is a progressive concentration of biological material within Carpens populations rather than diffuse recycling across the fluid environment.

Both of these dynamics — monomer interception and engulfment — foreshadow the predatory ecology that will define Captor lineages in the Photian Era, when the same mechanisms are applied to living targets under the intensified competitive pressures of a surface-exposed biosphere.

### Evolution
[[Sulphovita]] Captor descended from peripheral [[Sulphovita Primalis Purus]] populations at the boundaries of high density Primalis zones. The selective pressure was monomer scarcity — sustained Primalis population density consuming free amino acids faster than abiotic synthesis replenished them created a resource gradient at population boundaries where conventional [[Sulphovita]] replication became increasingly inefficient.

Dying organisms at these boundaries attempted peptide repair with insufficient monomer resources, producing fragmented partially-assembled peptide structures rather than functional motifs. These fragments accumulated in the surrounding fluid, representing a novel resource pool with no existing consumer. The first Captor lineage — Legens — emerged as surface-bound cleavage modules appeared in Purus populations already stressed by monomer scarcity, allowing fragment import through existing amino acid transport channels.

The divergence from Primalis Purus was gradual — cleavage module efficiency increasing across generations until fragment scavenging contributed meaningfully to monomer acquisition. There was no single divergence event but a progressive shift in populations already operating at the margins of free amino acid availability.

### The Cleavage and Import Mechanism
Baseline [[Sulphovita]] amino acid importers are sized and shaped for individual amino acids and short di/tripeptide fragments. Longer fragmented peptide structures from dying organisms cannot be imported directly — they exceed the conformational matching threshold of existing import modules.

Captor resolves this through a **surface-bound cleavage module** — a membrane-embedded peptide module operating at the outer membrane surface rather than within the transport channel itself.

The mechanism proceeds in three stages:

**Stage 1 — Fragment Binding**
Fragmented peptide structures in the surrounding fluid contact the outer membrane surface and bind to the cleavage module through electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions. The cleavage module's conformation is broadly complementary to peptide backbone chemistry rather than specific to any particular sequence, allowing it to bind a wide range of fragment types regardless of their original functional identity.

**Stage 2 — Cleavage**
The bound fragment is cleaved into segments of appropriate length for existing amino acid import channels. Cleavage occurs through peptide bond hydrolysis catalysed by the module's active site. In Legens this process is inefficient — cleavage is imprecise, producing segments of variable length, some too large for import and lost back to the fluid. In Carpens cleavage precision is significantly improved, producing consistently importable segments with minimal loss.

**Stage 3 — Import**
Cleaved segments are passed directly to adjacent amino acid import modules and transported inward to the monomer pool. The direct handoff between cleavage module and import module minimises diffusion loss between steps — a spatial coordination analogous to Condensus's sulphur oxidation module clustering but operating at the membrane surface rather than within the peptide network interior.

### Engulfment Mechanism
Present in [[Sulphovita Captor Carpens]] only.

Engulfment is the extension and fusion of the sulphonolipid membrane around an external target, enclosing it within a membrane-bounded internal compartment — an **engulfment vacuole** — where its contents can be processed directly without diffusion loss to the surrounding fluid.

The mechanism is mechanistically related to membrane fission in reverse — where fission constricts and separates membrane material, engulfment extends and fuses it around a target.

#### The Process
**Stage 1 — Target Recognition**
Carpens detects recently dead [[Sulphovita]] organisms through chemical signatures — specifically the elevated concentration of degrading peptide material and membrane lipid breakdown products diffusing outward from a post-death organism in the days-to-weeks pre-rupture window. These chemical gradients provide a directional signal that biases Carpens membrane extension toward the target.

**Stage 2 — Membrane Extension**
The sulphonolipid membrane extends outward toward the target, driven by localised lipid synthesis at the extension front. The extension is not directed movement of the whole organism but a localised membrane growth response — Carpens remains largely stationary while its membrane reaches toward the target.

**Stage 3 — Engulfment and Fusion**
The extending membrane surrounds the target organism and fuses at the contact point, enclosing it within an internal vacuole. The target's membrane and the vacuole membrane are now in direct contact within Carpens's internal environment.

**Stage 4 — Processing**
The enclosed target's membrane degrades within the vacuole — accelerated by the internal chemical environment and proximity to Carpens's own peptide network. Fragmented peptide contents are released directly into the vacuole fluid and processed by cleavage modules lining the vacuole interior, with cleaved segments passed to the internal monomer pool. Lipid material from the target membrane is incorporated into Carpens's own membrane reserves.

#### Limitations on Living Targets
Engulfment of living [[Sulphovita]] is possible in principle — the mechanism does not distinguish between living and dead targets at the recognition stage. However living organisms actively maintain membrane integrity and resist engulfment through continuous membrane repair. Carpens membrane extension against a living target is slower than the target's repair rate under most conditions — engulfment stalls, consuming Carpens membrane synthesis resources without successfully enclosing the target. The net effect on a healthy living target is a minor resource drain on both organisms rather than successful predation.

This limitation is specific to the Erebian Era chemical environment where organisms are well-resourced enough to maintain membrane repair. The Photian Era, with its environmental disruption and resource competition, will produce conditions where living targets are sufficiently stressed that membrane repair rates drop below Carpens's engulfment rate — the threshold at which Captor transitions from scavenger to predator.
