### Overview
[[Ferrovita Firmus]] Tactus is the second lineage of [[Ferrovita Firmus]], emerging from [[Ferrovita Firmus Rudis]] populations through lattice defect variation producing sufficient electron accumulation response in juveniles to generate meaningful electrophoretic drift. Its single innovation — directed juvenile navigation toward Fe³⁺-rich rock — provides an immediate and significant survival advantage over Rudis in zones where suitable rock surfaces are not densely packed. Adults retain the permanent rock attachment of Rudis entirely — Tactus is a juvenile survival improvement, not an adult mobility gain.

### Environmental Niche
- **Location:** Vent core zones of [[Hydrology#Pocket A|Pocket A]] where Fe³⁺-rich rock surfaces are present but not immediately adjacent to budding parents — moderate rock surface density where directed juvenile navigation meaningfully improves establishment rates
- **Temperature:** ~200–250°C, consistent with [[Ferrovita Firmus]] range
- **Pressure:** Extreme — deepest subsurface zones
- **Chemistry:** Consistent with [[Ferrovita Firmus#Environmental Niche]]

### Electrophoresis and Navigation
Follows [[Ferrovita#Electrophoresis and Navigation]] entirely.

Electrophoresis in Tactus is a juvenile-only mechanism. Electron accumulation from excess H₂ input relative to scarce dissolved Fe³⁺ generates a net negative charge across the juvenile's pyrite shell, producing passive drift toward the strongest positive electrochemical potential in the vicinity — Fe³⁺-rich rock surfaces. Juvenile survival rates are dramatically higher than [[Ferrovita Firmus Rudis]] in zones where rock surfaces are not immediately adjacent to budding parents.

Adult Tactus organisms are as sessile as Rudis once established on rock. Adult electrophoresis is present but produces no meaningful drift under normal metabolic conditions — any charge accumulation in adults is more indicative of approaching dormancy than a functional navigational state.

### Ecological Character
Tactus occupies zones of moderate rock surface density within Pocket A's vent cores — environments where Rudis juvenile chance-settlement succeeds at insufficient rates to sustain competitive populations but where directed juvenile navigation closes the gap between bud production and successful establishment decisively.

In the most Fe³⁺-rich vent core zones where Rudis remains viable through chance-settlement alone, Rudis and Tactus coexist without strong competitive exclusion. Tactus's advantage compounds progressively as rock surface spacing increases — the wider the gap between parent and available rock, the greater the survival differential between directed and undirected juvenile navigation.

Tactus is outcompeted by [[Ferrovita Firmus Mobilis]] in zones where local Fe³⁺ depletion is a recurring pressure — Tactus has no response to depleted rock other than dormancy, whereas Mobilis detaches and relocates. In stable high Fe³⁺ zones where depletion is rare, Tactus and Mobilis coexist without strong exclusion.

### Evolution
[[Ferrovita Firmus]] Tactus emerges from [[Ferrovita Firmus Rudis]] populations at approximately 600 Million AC as lattice defect variation produces sufficient electron accumulation response to generate meaningful juvenile electrophoretic drift. The divergence is gradual — populations in zones of moderate rock surface density accumulating selective pressure toward improved juvenile navigation across many generations.

Tactus gives rise to [[Ferrovita Firmus Mobilis]] through further defect variation extending the electrophoretic mechanism into adult capability and developing the detachment mechanism. The divergence is documented in [[Ferrovita Firmus Mobilis]].

Tactus itself persists across the Erebian Era in stable high Fe³⁺ zones where adult mobility provides insufficient additional advantage to drive Mobilis to dominance.
