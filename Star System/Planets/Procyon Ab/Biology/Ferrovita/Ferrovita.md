### Overview
Ferrovita are extremophilic, mineral-based lifeforms inhabiting the deepest hydrothermal vent zones of [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]]. Existing at the boundary between biology and [[Geology]], they are not built from organic polymers but from structured iron–sulphur minerals. Their bodies grow directly from the surrounding rock, forming slow-living, crystalline systems that extract energy from redox gradients in iron-rich fluids. To outside observers, Ferrovita are nearly indistinguishable from vent formations — living structures embedded within the planet’s crust.

### Genetics
Ferrovita encodes information not in molecular sequences but in the **three dimensional pattern of electrochemical properties across its iron-sulphur lattice**. This system has no analogue in carbon-based life — genetics, metabolism, and structure are not separate systems but a single unified electrochemical phenomenon.

#### Information Storage
A perfect FeS₂ crystal lattice has every atom in precise geometric arrangement, conducting electrons uniformly. Ferrovita's lattice is never perfect — it is defined by controlled imperfection. Four categories of defect carry information:

**Vacancy defects** — missing atoms creating gaps in the conductive network. These interrupt electron flow pathways, functioning analogously to open switches in a circuit.

**Substitution defects** — trace metal ions from Pocket A fluid, primarily nickel, copper, and zinc, substituting for iron at specific lattice positions. Each substitution alters the electrochemical properties of that node, changing how electrons move through the surrounding region.

**Oxidation state variations** — Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ distributed at specific positions throughout the lattice rather than uniformly. Since Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ have different electron configurations they create localised regions of differing conductivity, functioning as variable resistances within the circuit.

**Geometric distortions** — atoms slightly displaced from ideal positions by mechanical stress or thermal fluctuation, subtly altering bond angles and therefore electron orbital overlap across affected regions.

The collective three dimensional map of these defects determines the precise pattern of electron flow pathways through the entire lattice — which regions conduct strongly, which resist, which redirect. This pattern is Ferrovita's genome. Different patterns produce different metabolic behaviours, different growth tendencies, and different catalytic properties at specific lattice regions. The organism is simultaneously a battery, a circuit board, and a self-replicating mineral — metabolism and genetics are not merely linked but are the same physical phenomenon expressed differently.

#### Defect Propagation — Inheritance
New lattice material accretes throughout the entire structure simultaneously. Iron is supplied as Fe²⁺ from rock reduction at the rock-facing interface, moving inward. Sulphur is supplied from H₂S diffusing passively through microfractures from Pocket A fluid, moving inward from the fluid-facing side. These two streams meet and combine across the whole lattice, crystallising new FeS₂ wherever local conditions permit.

Existing defects act as crystallisation templates — the irregular geometry of the existing lattice guides how new material crystallises around it. Vacancies in the existing structure create regions of altered surface energy that bias new crystal growth toward similar vacancy formation nearby. Substitution defects alter local chemistry in ways that favour incorporation of the same trace metals into adjacent new material. Oxidation state patterns propagate because existing Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ distributions influence the redox state of newly incorporated iron.

New lattice growth therefore inherits the existing defect pattern with reasonable fidelity — not through any active copying mechanism but through the passive physical and chemical influence of existing structure on new crystallisation. The circuit replicates itself by being the template its own growth follows.

#### Variation Sources
Inheritance fidelity is high but not absolute. Four mechanisms introduce variation:

**Uneven iron supply** — iron reduction at the rock interface is spatially variable, producing regions of faster and slower Fe²⁺ supply. Areas of iron scarcity during growth produce vacancy defects at higher rates than the parent pattern specifies, altering local electron flow.

**Uneven sulphur supply** — H₂S diffusion through microfractures is not uniform across the shell surface. Regions of lower sulphur availability during crystallisation produce sulphur vacancy defects or incorporate alternative sulphur compounds, distorting the inherited pattern locally.

**Temperature fluctuations** — Pocket A's 150–250°C temperature range causes thermal expansion and contraction of the lattice. Fluctuations during active growth alter crystallisation geometry, introducing geometric distortions not present in the parent structure.

**Trace metal availability** — substitution defects depend on which trace metals are present in Pocket A fluid at the moment of crystallisation. Fluctuations in nickel, copper, or zinc concentration produce different substitution patterns than the parent lattice, altering electron flow properties at affected nodes.

#### Replication Speed
Lattice growth is inherently slow — constrained by iron reduction rate at the rock interface, H₂S diffusion rate through the shell, and the thermodynamics of crystal growth at 150–250°C. Full organism replication through budding operates on timescales of **years to centuries** depending on local chemical availability and organism scale. This is orders of magnitude slower than [[Sulphovita]]'s days to weeks replication cycle, reflecting Ferrovita's fundamental character — stable, ancient, and slow relative to all life that will eventually descend from [[Sulphovita]]'s lineage.

### Membrane
- **Outer boundary:** Porous pyrite (FeS₂) composite shell
- **Form:** Irregular crystalline masses, veins, or nodular growths
- **Scale:** Microscopic seeds to metre-scale colonies
- **Appearance:** Metallic gold to dark bronze, visually continuous with surrounding rock
The pyrite shell is an active mineral interface rather than a passive barrier. The same electron transport network that drives Ferrovita's metabolism extends into and regulates the shell itself — creating electrochemical gradients that actively pull Fe³⁺ inward while expelling waste products outward. Neutral molecules — primarily H₂ and H₂S from Pocket A fluid — diffuse inward passively through microfractures and lattice discontinuities, supplementing the actively transported materials.

The shell grows simultaneously at two interfaces. At the fluid-facing surface new pyrite accretes from dissolved iron in Pocket A fluid, expanding outward. At the rock-facing surface the shell integrates directly with Fe³⁺-rich vent rock, growing inward as iron is consumed and the underlying mineral structure destabilises. Growth is uneven across both surfaces — regions of higher chemical gradient accrete faster, producing an irregular, asymmetric shell morphology.

This uneven growth is the foundation of Ferrovita's reproductive mechanism. Regions of accelerated accrual form outward protrusions — buds — that develop their own internal lattice structure and redox gradients inherited from the parent. At sufficient mass a bud generates mechanical stress at its connection point with the parent shell. When this stress exceeds the structural integrity of the connecting material the bud separates, settling nearby and attaching to available rock surface to begin independent growth. See [[Ferrovita Firmus]] and [[Ferrovita Novus]] for sub-lineage specific variations on this process.

Structurally the organism remains inseparable from its substrate — the rock-facing surface and the rock itself are chemically continuous at the attachment interface. Ferrovita does not sit on rock. At its base it becomes rock, consuming and replacing mineral structure as it grows.
### Metabolism
Ferrovita's metabolism is founded on a single core reaction — iron reduction using hydrogen as the primary electron donor:

**H₂ + 2Fe³⁺ → 2Fe²⁺ + 2H⁺**

Unlike carbon-based metabolic systems where reactions occur through molecular pathways in solution, Ferrovita's metabolism occurs across its entire solid-state lattice simultaneously, creating a biological semiconductor. Electron transport is a structural phenomenon — the lattice itself is the metabolic machinery, with the defect pattern determining exactly how energy moves through the organism. Metabolism and genetics are therefore not merely linked but physically inseparable.

#### Stepwise Mechanism
**Step 1 — H₂ Import and Activation**
H₂ diffuses passively through microfractures in the pyrite shell into the lattice interior. At specific lattice regions where defect geometry creates the appropriate electrochemical environment, H₂ undergoes oxidative splitting — the molecule is cleaved into two protons and two electrons. The precise configuration of defects at these activation sites determines their catalytic efficiency, linking genetic information directly to metabolic performance.

**Step 2 — Electron Injection**
The two electrons released from H₂ splitting are injected directly into the conductive lattice network at the activation site. Unlike enzymatic systems where electrons travel through molecular carriers in solution, Ferrovita's electrons enter a solid-state conductive network and travel through mineral structure rather than fluid.

**Step 3 — Electron Transport**
Electrons move through the lattice from regions of lower electrochemical potential toward regions of higher potential — specifically toward Fe³⁺ rich zones. The defect pattern functions as a circuit, determining exactly which pathways electrons follow, which regions receive priority supply, and how energy is distributed across the structure. Vacancy defects act as resistances, substitution defects alter local conductivity, and oxidation state variations create electrochemical gradients that direct electron flow. The organism's genetic identity and its metabolic behaviour are expressed through the same physical phenomenon.

**Step 4 — Fe³⁺ Reduction**
Electrons arrive at Fe³⁺ reduction sites and complete the core reaction — Fe³⁺ accepts electrons and is reduced to Fe²⁺. Energy is released at this step and captured not in molecular bonds but in maintained electrochemical gradients across the lattice structure. These gradients drive further growth, shell accrual, and bud development.

**Step 5 — Proton Expulsion**
The two protons produced during H₂ activation are expelled outward through the shell into surrounding fluid. Continuous proton expulsion slightly acidifies the immediate fluid environment around the organism, creating a localised chemical signature that distinguishes actively metabolising Ferrovita from inert mineral formations.

#### Energy Storage and Use
Ferrovita does not store energy in molecules — there are no ATP equivalents, no chemical energy reserves. Energy exists only as maintained electrochemical gradients across the lattice. When metabolic activity slows these gradients decay, reducing the organism's capacity for growth and reproduction. When activity is high gradients steepen, accelerating lattice accrual and bud development.

This means Ferrovita has no energy buffer — it is entirely dependent on continuous Fe³⁺ availability and H₂ supply. Interruption of either substrate causes immediate metabolic slowdown rather than a buffered response. Given the geological stability of Pocket A's vent chemistry this is rarely a problem during the Erebian Era, but represents a fundamental vulnerability that will become significant during post-capture environmental transitions.

#### Waste Products and Ecological Implications
The primary waste products of Ferrovita metabolism are:
- **Fe²⁺** — released into surrounding fluid, significantly enriching the already iron-saturated Pocket A environment
- **H⁺** — continuous proton expulsion maintaining local acidity
- **H₂O** — produced as Fe²⁺ and expelled protons react with available oxygen compounds in fluid

Fe²⁺ accumulation in Pocket A fluid represents the most significant ecological output of Ferrovita metabolism — continuously enriching the iron chemistry of the vent environment and maintaining the strongly reducing conditions that define Pocket A. This output will have significant implications for the broader Xenian biosphere during the Photian Era when Ferrovita and [[Sulphovita]] radices come into contact.
### Reproduction
Ferrovita reproduces through **asymmetric budding** — a process emerging directly from the physics of bidirectional shell growth rather than any active reproductive mechanism. Reproduction is inseparable from growth; the organism does not initiate reproduction as a distinct biological event but instead buds as an inevitable consequence of how it builds itself.

#### Bud Formation
The pyrite shell accretes simultaneously at two interfaces — inward at the rock-facing surface as Fe³⁺ is consumed from the substrate, and outward at the fluid-facing surface as dissolved iron and sulphur from Pocket A fluid crystallise onto the exterior. Growth rate is spatially uneven across both surfaces, governed by local chemical availability, temperature variation, and the existing defect pattern's influence on crystallisation.

Regions of accelerated accrual develop outward protrusions — buds. As a bud grows it develops its own internal lattice structure, with new FeS₂ crystallising around the inherited defect pattern propagated from the parent. The bud's internal redox gradients establish progressively as its lattice matures, eventually reaching sufficient complexity to sustain independent metabolic activity.

#### Defect Inheritance and Variation
The bud inherits its parent's defect pattern through the same templated crystallisation mechanism that governs all Ferrovita growth. However bud formation introduces variation at rates significantly higher than normal growth — the geometry of the connection point between bud and parent creates mechanical stress that distorts crystallisation, uneven iron and sulphur supply to the developing bud produces substitution and vacancy defects not present in the parent, and thermal fluctuations during the extended bud development period compound these effects.

Bud inheritance is therefore characterised by **more variation than fidelity** — daughter organisms are recognisably derived from their parent's electrochemical architecture but carry meaningful differences in their defect patterns. These differences translate directly into different metabolic behaviours, growth tendencies, and environmental tolerances, driving diversification across Ferrovita populations over geological timescales.

#### Separation
At sufficient mass the bud generates mechanical stress at its connection point with the parent shell that exceeds the structural integrity of the connecting pyrite material. The connection fractures and the bud separates as an independent organism.

Separation is not actively controlled — it occurs when physical forces dictate. The timing is therefore variable, influenced by bud growth rate, parent shell thickness at the connection point, fluid turbulence, and thermal expansion cycles. A single parent organism may carry multiple buds at different stages of development simultaneously, separating sequentially as each reaches sufficient mass.

#### Juvenile Phase
Following separation the juvenile Ferrovita enters a transitional phase before rock attachment is established. During this period it relies entirely on dissolved Fe³⁺ from Pocket A fluid — a scarce resource at 2–8 mmol/L compared to the concentrated Fe³⁺ available at rock surfaces. Metabolic activity is reduced but not absent, sustained by whatever dissolved Fe³⁺ the juvenile can access combined with passively diffusing H₂.

The juvenile phase ends upon successful attachment to a Fe³⁺ rich rock surface, at which point full metabolic activity resumes and growth begins in earnest. Mortality during the juvenile phase is high — the combination of reduced metabolic capacity, dense pyrite mass under 2.4g gravity, and dependence on scarce dissolved Fe³⁺ means many juveniles fail to establish before their electrochemical gradients decay below the threshold required to sustain the attachment process. Ferrovita compensates through continuous bud production over geological timescales rather than through juvenile survival efficiency.

#### Growth Rate
Established Ferrovita grows extremely slowly — lattice accrual is constrained by iron reduction rate, H₂ diffusion, and crystal growth thermodynamics at 150–250°C. Individual organisms may take **decades to centuries** to reach sizes sufficient for bud production. Full reproductive cycles — from bud initiation to juvenile attachment and establishment — operate on timescales of **centuries to millennia**.

This makes Ferrovita evolution extraordinarily slow relative to [[Sulphovita]], whose reproductive cycle completes in days to weeks. Over the billion years of the Erebian Era however even Ferrovita's glacial reproductive rate produces many generations of accumulated variation, particularly in the defect patterns governing electron flow and Fe³⁺ reduction efficiency.

### Dormancy and Death
Ferrovita does not die through biological decay. As a mineral structure with no organic components there is no intrinsic degradation mechanism — a Ferrovita organism that ceases metabolic activity simply stops, its pyrite lattice persisting indefinitely in whatever state it occupied at the moment of cessation.

#### Dormancy
When chemical substrates fall below the threshold required to maintain electrochemical gradients — due to vent inactivity, Fe³⁺ depletion, or H₂ scarcity — Ferrovita enters dormancy. Metabolic activity slows progressively as gradients decay, eventually reaching a state of complete electrochemical stillness. The lattice structure remains physically intact and the defect pattern is fully preserved.

Dormancy has no maximum duration. A dormant Ferrovita is chemically inert but structurally complete — residual electrochemical gradients, however decayed, persist within the lattice indefinitely as long as the physical structure remains intact. If suitable chemistry returns, even after geological timescales of dormancy, these residual gradients can seed metabolic recovery. Individual Ferrovita organisms are therefore potentially immortal — the oldest Firmus individuals may have persisted since the earliest Erebian Era, surviving multiple vent cycles through repeated dormancy and recovery.

A dormant Ferrovita is physically indistinguishable from dead Ferrovita or inert rock. Without active electron transport no chemical signature distinguishes it from its surroundings.

#### Death
True death — irreversible cessation — occurs only through three mechanisms:

**Structural destruction** — geological activity, mechanical stress, or competitive displacement physically shatters the lattice. If fragmentation is severe enough that no region retains sufficient structural continuity to maintain even residual gradients, recovery is impossible. Fragments below a critical size threshold cannot sustain the electrochemical complexity required for metabolic recovery and are functionally dead, becoming inert mineral matter indistinguishable from surrounding rock.

**Permanent vent extinction** — if the vent system supplying Fe³⁺ and H₂ closes permanently with no recovery, gradients decay to absolute zero over sufficient time. Once residual gradients fully dissipate the templating information encoded in the defect pattern loses its functional context — the circuit exists but carries no current and cannot be restarted without an external electrochemical seed. This threshold is the point of true death. 

**Competitive displacement** — another Ferrovita organism grows over and physically integrates with a smaller organism, subsuming its lattice into its own structure. The displaced organism's defect pattern may partially persist within the larger organism's lattice as an anomalous region, a fossil of genetic information absorbed rather than destroyed.
#### Post-Death Persistence 
A dead Ferrovita remains as an intact pyrite mineral structure embedded in rock or vent substrate. It does not decompose. The lattice defect pattern is physically preserved in the mineral indefinitely — a fossil from the moment of death, carrying no functional information but retaining the geometric record of the organism's electrochemical history. Over geological timescales these structures become incorporated into the rock record of [[Procyon Ab|Xenos]], indistinguishable from abiotic mineral formations except to analysis capable of identifying the non-random defect distributions characteristic of biological lattice growth.

### Evolutionary Overview
Ferrovita's evolutionary history across the Erebian Era is a sequence of incremental innovations, each conferring a meaningful survival advantage over the previous grade. Four grades are recognised within the Ferrovita radix, progressing from the original sessile iron reducers through to the fully mobile methanogenic Novus lineage.

The lineage begins with **Ferrovita Rudis** — the most primitive grade, relying entirely on chance for juvenile establishment. From Rudis, two sequential innovations produce **Ferrovita Tactus** and **Ferrovita Mobilis**, each improving survival and resource access while retaining iron reduction as the sole metabolism. At Mobilis the lineage diverges — the majority retain iron reduction as their defining metabolic strategy, remaining within the **[[Ferrovita Firmus]]** domain, while a population developing methanogenic capacity branches into **[[Ferrovita Novus]]**. Novus subsequently diverges into **Novus Parcus** and **Novus Liber**, completing the Erebian Era's biological diversification within the Ferrovita radix.

Each grade is documented in detail within the relevant sub-lineage notes. The full evolutionary sequence represents approximately one billion years of accumulated variation under the selective pressures of [[Hydrology#Pocket A|Pocket A]]'s chemically aggressive and spatially constrained environment.
